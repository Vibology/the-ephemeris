# Imago Dei: The God-Image in the Human Psyche

## Overview

### Jung's Definition

**Imago Dei** (Latin: "image of God") is Jung's term for the God-image—not God as theological reality, but **the psychological experience of the divine** as it manifests within the human psyche. It is an **autonomous archetypal content**, numinous and capable of overwhelming consciousness, that represents the experience of totality, transcendence, and the sacred.

In *Psychology and Religion* (CW 11, par. 142), Jung writes[^1]:

> "The God-image in man was not invented, it was experienced. It is an autonomous psychic content, and numinous—i.e., it is capable of overpowering the conscious mind."

Jung is careful to distinguish between **God as metaphysical reality** (which he refuses to claim knowledge of) and **the God-image as empirical psychological fact** (which he observes universally across cultures and throughout history). In *Answer to Job* (CW 11), he writes[^2]:

> "Strictly speaking, the God-image does not coincide with the unconscious as such, but with a special content of it, namely the archetype of the self. It is this archetype from which we can no longer distinguish the God-image empirically."

The Imago Dei is thus **functionally equivalent to the archetype of [[The Self]]**—both represent:
- **Wholeness and totality** (the union of conscious and unconscious)
- **The union of opposites** (*complexio oppositorum*)
- **The numinous** (the mysterium tremendum et fascinans)
- **The goal of individuation** (the completest expression of individuality)

The critical difference: the **Self** is Jung's psychological term; the **Imago Dei** is the same reality experienced as **divine**—the God within, the sacred center, the transcendent function.

### The God-Image as Autonomous Archetype

Jung insists that the God-image is **not a projection of human wishes or fears** but an **autonomous psychic reality** that emerges from the collective unconscious. It appears in dreams, visions, and numinous experiences without being willed or created by the ego. It is **objective**—it happens to consciousness rather than being produced by consciousness.

In *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, Jung recounts his childhood vision of God defecating on Basel Cathedral—a shocking, blasphemous image that came unbidden and forced him to recognize that **the God-image contains darkness as well as light**[^3]. This recognition became foundational to his later work.

The God-image is:
- **Numinous:** It evokes awe, terror, fascination (Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans*)
- **Autonomous:** It possesses the ego rather than being possessed by it
- **Supraordinate:** It is greater than the ego and cannot be controlled
- **Transformative:** Encounters with it fundamentally alter consciousness

### The Paradox of the God-Image: Light and Shadow

Jung's most controversial contribution: **the God-image contains both light and dark, good and evil**. This is not a theological claim about God's nature but a **psychological observation**: the God-image as experienced in the psyche is **not purely good**. It includes the Terrible, the Wrathful, the Destructive.

In *Answer to Job*, Jung analyzes the Old Testament God who is simultaneously:
- **Creator and Destroyer**
- **Merciful and Wrathful**
- **Just and Capricious**
- **Loving and Jealous**

Jung writes[^4]:

> "Yahweh is not split but an antinomy—a totality of inner opposites—and this is the indispensable condition for his tremendous dynamism, his omniscience and omnipotence."

The biblical God torments Job (a righteous man) without cause, makes a wager with Satan, and refuses to explain or justify his actions. Jung sees this as a **representation of the unconscious God-image**—not the sanitized, all-good deity of systematic theology, but the **raw numinous experience** of the divine that includes terror, mystery, and moral ambiguity.

**Psychological Implication:** The God-image, like [[The Self]], is the **union of all opposites**. It contains:
- Light and shadow
- Good and evil
- Creation and destruction
- Order and chaos

To encounter the Imago Dei is to confront **totality**—not merely the light side that consciousness prefers, but the **dark side** that the ego would rather deny.

## The Projection and Withdrawal of the God-Image

### Historical Evolution

Jung traces the **withdrawal of projections** throughout human history—the gradual recognition that the divine is not "out there" but **within the psyche**.

**Stage 1: Animism (Nature as Divine)**
- The God-image is projected onto **natural phenomena**: sun, moon, thunder, rivers, animals.
- The world is alive with numinous presence—everything is ensouled.
- Consciousness is immersed in **participation mystique** with nature.

**Stage 2: Polytheism (Deities as Distinct)**
- The God-image differentiates into **multiple gods and goddesses**, each representing a specific archetypal force (love, war, wisdom, death).
- Greek/Roman, Norse, Hindu pantheons embody this stage.
- The gods are **personified projections** of psychic contents.

**Stage 3: Monotheism (One God Above)**
- The God-image withdraws from nature and multiple deities into a **single transcendent deity** (Yahweh, Allah, God the Father).
- The divine is no longer immanent in the world but **beyond it** (Heaven, the transcendent).
- Consciousness differentiates further—the world is **desacralized**, and God is **wholly other**.

**Stage 4: Institutional Religion (Church as Mediator)**
- The God-image is projected onto **the Church** (or Temple, Mosque, Synagogue).
- The institution becomes the **container and dispenser of the divine**—salvation comes through sacraments, clergy, dogma.
- Consciousness outsources the numinous to **external authority**.

**Stage 5: Modern Disenchantment (Death of God)**
- Nietzsche's proclamation: "God is dead"—the withdrawal of the God-image from external institutions.
- Secularization, atheism, loss of collective religious symbols.
- Crisis: **where does the numinous go when the projections are withdrawn?**

**Stage 6: Interiority (The God Within)**
- Jung's contribution: The God-image **returns to the psyche** where it originated.
- The divine is **not destroyed** but **interiorized**—it is recognized as the **Self**, the archetype of wholeness within.
- Individuation becomes the **new religious function**—the conscious relationship with the God-image within.

Jung writes in *Psychology and Alchemy* (CW 12, par. 11)[^5]:

> "The gods have become diseases; Zeus no longer rules Olympus but rather the solar plexus, and produces curious specimens for the doctor's consulting room."

**Psychological Meaning:** When projections are withdrawn, the archetypal energies (formerly carried by gods or institutions) **erupt from the unconscious** as neurosis, compulsion, possession. The modern individual must **consciously relate to the God-image** or be overwhelmed by it.

## Practical Application

### Recognition in Life

**How do you know the Imago Dei is constellated?**

1. **Numinous Experiences:**
   - Overwhelming awe or terror in the presence of beauty, death, or mystery
   - Synchronicities that feel **orchestrated by something beyond the ego**
   - Peak experiences (Maslow) where the boundaries of self dissolve
   - Near-death experiences or mystical visions
   - Encounters with great art, music, nature, or sacred texts that shatter the ego

2. **Dreams of the Divine:**
   - Figures of supraordinate authority: Christ, Buddha, gods, radiant beings
   - Mandalas and quaternity symbols (four gates, four corners, the cross)
   - Cosmic imagery: stars, suns, the cosmos, infinite space
   - Light or darkness of overwhelming intensity
   - The voice of God or an authoritative presence commanding or guiding

3. **Projection onto External Authorities:**
   - Guru worship, idealization of spiritual teachers
   - Political messianism (the leader as savior)
   - Idolization of romantic partners (the beloved as divine)
   - Fundamentalism (the text or institution as infallible)

4. **Crisis and the Dark Night:**
   - Loss of faith, collapse of meaning, despair
   - The "death of God" experienced personally
   - Spiritual emergency (Grof) where the ego is overwhelmed by transpersonal content

### Active Imagination and Engagement

**Dialogue with the God-Image:**

Jung engaged the God-image through **active imagination**, particularly in his encounters with **Philemon** (a wise old man figure Jung met in visions). Philemon taught Jung that **thoughts have an autonomous life**—they are not created by the ego but emerge from the Self.

1. **Enter the Sacred Precinct:**
   - Meditation, prayer, or ritual creates the container for encounter.
   - Visualize a **sacred space**: temple, mountaintop, throne room, cosmic void.

2. **Invoke the Presence:**
   - Do not demand or control. **Wait** for the God-image to appear in its own form.
   - It may appear as a figure (wise elder, radiant child, divine king), a symbol (cross, mandala, light), or a presence without form.

3. **Dialogue:**
   - Ask: *"What do you require of me? What is the meaning of my life? What must I surrender?"*
   - Listen without interpreting. The God-image speaks in **symbols, images, and felt sense**, not concepts.

4. **Record the Encounter:**
   - Write, draw, or paint what was revealed.
   - Jung painted mandalas daily to track his inner state and the movement of the Self.

**Warning:** Do not **identify** with the God-image (inflation). Jung writes: *"The self is not the ego. The self is the totality of the psyche."* The ego must remain **distinct** while serving the Self.

### Integration Practices

**Avoiding Inflation (Ego-Self Identification):**

The greatest danger: **claiming to be God** rather than recognizing the God-image as **within but not identical to the ego**. Inflation leads to:
- Messianic delusions ("I am the chosen one")
- Guru complexes ("I am enlightened, beyond ego")
- Psychotic breaks (loss of ego boundaries)

**The Corrective:**
- Maintain the **ego-Self axis**—a relationship, not fusion.
- Jung: *"The ego is the object; the Self is the subject."*
- Humility: *"I contain the God-image, but I am not God."*
- Stay grounded in the **body, relationships, daily life**.

**Accepting the Dark Side of the God-Image:**

The God-image is **not purely good**. Jung's controversial insistence: **God contains evil**, or at minimum, the God-image as experienced in the psyche includes destructive, amoral, and terrifying aspects.

**Practical Steps:**
1. **Read *Answer to Job*:** Confront Jung's analysis of Yahweh's shadow.
2. **Acknowledge Divine Wrath:** When life shatters you (illness, loss, betrayal), recognize the **dark face of God**—not as punishment but as the Terrible Mother/Father aspect of the divine.
3. **Stop Splitting:** Do not project the dark side onto "Satan" or "evil forces." The Imago Dei is **totality**—it includes destruction as well as creation.
4. **Gnostic Abraxas:** Jung saw Abraxas (the Gnostic deity who is both God and Devil, light and dark) as a more psychologically complete symbol than the purely good Christ.

**Ritual and Sacred Practice:**

Creating **vertical connection** to the Imago Dei:
- **Daily Prayer or Meditation:** Not as petition but as **relationship**—speaking to the divine within.
- **Sacred Reading:** Engaging scripture, poetry, or myth as **living symbol** rather than historical text.
- **Pilgrimage or Retreat:** Physical withdrawal to create space for the numinous.
- **Art as Devotion:** Painting, music, dance as offerings to the God-image.

### In Relationships and Projection

**The God-Image Projected onto Others:**

Before the God-image is consciously integrated, it is **projected outward**:

**Romantic Love:**
- Falling in love constellates the Anima/Animus as a **carrier of the God-image**.
- The beloved appears **divine**—luminous, perfect, the answer to all longing.
- Danger: When the projection collapses, the beloved becomes the **devil** (shadow God-image).
- Integration: Recognize the beloved **triggered** the God-image but is not its source. Withdraw the projection and relate as equals.

**Spiritual Teachers:**
- Students project the Imago Dei onto gurus, priests, therapists.
- The teacher appears to possess **ultimate wisdom or divine authority**.
- Danger: If the teacher accepts the projection (inflation), exploitation follows (sexual, financial, psychological).
- Integration: The healthy teacher **reflects the projection back**: *"What you see in me is in you."*

**Collective Projections:**
- Nations project the Imago Dei onto **the Fatherland** (nationalism).
- Political movements project it onto **the Leader** (totalitarianism).
- Religions project it onto **the Institution** (church as sole mediator of grace).
- Danger: Collective inflation leads to **holy wars, crusades, genocides**—evil committed in the name of the Good.

**Withdrawal as Individuation:**
- The mature individual **withdraws the God-image from external objects** and recognizes it as **within**.
- This does not destroy relationship—it **deepens** it. Love becomes meeting between two wholes, not fusion with the divine other.

## Jungian Analysis

### 1. Jung's Theory Deep Dive

**The Imago Dei vs. Theological God:**

Jung carefully distinguishes his work from theology:
- **Theology** makes metaphysical claims about God's existence, nature, and will.
- **Psychology** observes the **God-image** as it appears in the psyche—dreams, visions, projections, beliefs.

Jung writes in *Answer to Job*[^6]:

> "I am not concerned with the question of whether God exists or not. I am concerned with the fact that the God-image exists in the human psyche, and that it has an enormous influence on human life."

This methodological distinction allows Jung to treat the God-image as an **empirical phenomenon** without making theological assertions. He is **agnostic** about the metaphysical God but **certain** about the psychological God-image.

**The Self as God-Image:**

Jung's most radical move: identifying the Self (the archetype of wholeness) with the Imago Dei. In *Psychology and Alchemy* (CW 12, par. 247), he writes[^7]:

> "Intellectually the self is no more than a psychological concept, a construct that serves to express an unknowable essence which we cannot grasp as such, since by definition it transcends our powers of comprehension. It might equally well be called the 'God within us.'"

The Self and the Imago Dei are **functionally equivalent**:
- Both are **numinous** (overwhelming, sacred)
- Both represent **totality** (conscious and unconscious)
- Both are **supraordinate** to the ego
- Both are the **goal of individuation**
- Both are **experienced as divine** or transcendent

**Distinction:** The **Self** is Jung's neutral scientific term. The **Imago Dei** is the same reality **clothed in religious language** and experienced as **the divine**.

**The Problem of Evil in the God-Image:**

Jung's most controversial work, *Answer to Job*, confronts the **moral ambiguity** of the biblical God. Yahweh is:
- **Jealous:** Demands exclusive worship, punishes apostasy
- **Wrathful:** Floods the earth, destroys cities, tests Abraham
- **Capricious:** Torments Job without cause, makes wagers with Satan
- **Amoral:** Does not justify his actions to humans

Jung interprets this not as a theological problem but as a **psychological truth**: the God-image as experienced in the unconscious is **not purely good**. It contains:
- **Shadow:** The destructive, vengeful, chaotic aspect
- **Amorality:** Beyond human categories of good and evil
- **Totality:** The union of light and dark, creation and destruction

Jung writes[^8]:

> "If Yahweh, as we would expect of a God, were really conscious of himself, he would, in view of the true facts of the case, at least have put an end to the panegyrics on his justice. But he is too unconscious to be moral. Morality presupposes consciousness."

**Psychological Implication:** The God-image is **unconscious of itself**—it acts with tremendous power but without self-reflection or moral restraint. This is precisely the nature of the **unconscious**: it is **amoral, autonomous, and overwhelming**.

**Integration:** The task of individuation is to bring **consciousness to the God-image**—not to worship it blindly, but to **relate to it consciously**, acknowledging both its light and dark aspects.

### 2. Cross-Cultural Manifestations

**Hinduism: Brahman and Atman**

The Hindu tradition offers the closest parallel to Jung's God-image. **Brahman** is the **ultimate reality**, the ground of all being, beyond all attributes (*nirguna*). **Atman** is the individual soul, the **Self within**.

The Upanishads proclaim: ***"Tat tvam asi"*** ("That art Thou")—the realization that Atman (the Self within) is **identical** with Brahman (the Absolute). Jung saw this as a **psychological truth**: the God-image within the psyche (the Self) is **not separate** from the ultimate reality.

The Hindu tradition also acknowledges the **dark side of the divine**: Kali (the Terrible Mother), Shiva (the Destroyer), the dance of creation and destruction. The divine is **totality**, not merely goodness.

**Buddhism: Sunyata and Buddha-Nature**

Buddhism presents a paradox: the doctrine of *anatta* (no-self) teaches that there is **no fixed, permanent ego**, yet the goal is the realization of **Buddha-nature** (the True Self). The **Dharmakaya** (Truth Body) is the ultimate reality, beyond form.

Jung saw the Buddha as a **symbol of the Self**—the one who has realized the God-image within and transcended the illusion of the separate ego. Enlightenment is **not annihilation** but the recognition of the **Self that was always present**.

**Taoism: The Tao**

The Tao is the **"Way"**—the ineffable, nameless ground of existence. Lao Tzu writes in the *Tao Te Ching*: *"The Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao."* This is identical to Jung's claim that the God-image is **unknowable**—it transcends conceptual categories.

The Taoist symbol of **Yin-Yang** is a mandala of the Self—the union of opposites (light/dark, masculine/feminine) in dynamic balance. The God-image is **totality**, not one-sidedness.

**Christianity: Christ and Abraxas**

Jung viewed **Christ** as a powerful symbol of the Self—the God-Man who unites divinity and humanity, heaven and earth. Christ's teaching *"I and the Father are one"* (John 10:30) expresses the **ego's surrender to the Self**.

However, Jung critiqued traditional Christianity for **splitting the God-image**: Christ is purely light, purely good; Satan is purely dark, purely evil. This creates a **false dichotomy** that denies the totality of the divine.

Jung saw the **Gnostic Abraxas** (a deity who is both God and Devil, light and dark, creator and destroyer) as a more psychologically complete symbol. Abraxas is the **complexio oppositorum**—the union of all opposites that represents the true God-image.

**Islam: Allah and the 99 Names**

In Islamic theology, **Allah** is both transcendent (*tanzih*—utterly beyond creation) and immanent (*tashbih*—present in all things). The **99 Names of Allah** include both merciful attributes (Al-Rahman, Al-Rahim) and terrifying attributes (Al-Qahhar, the Subduer; Al-Mumit, the Giver of Death).

This captures the **totality** of the God-image—both nurturing and destructive, both compassionate and wrathful.

**Gnosticism: Sophia and the Demiurge**

Gnostic cosmology presents a **complex God-image**: the unknowable **Pleroma** (divine fullness), **Sophia** (divine wisdom who falls and creates the material world), and the **Demiurge** (the false god who rules matter and believes himself supreme).

Jung saw the Gnostic myth as a **psychological allegory**: the Demiurge represents the **ego** (limited, inflated, believing itself ultimate), while Sophia represents the **soul's descent into matter** (incarnation, suffering) and her eventual **return to the Pleroma** (individuation, reunion with the Self).

### 3. The Dark Side of the God-Image

**Why Jung Insists on the Shadow of God:**

Traditional theology (especially Christian) presents God as **purely good, purely light, purely loving**. Evil is attributed to:
- **Satan** (the fallen angel)
- **Human sin** (the Fall)
- **The absence of good** (Augustine's *privatio boni*)

Jung rejects this as **psychologically dishonest**. He argues:

1. **The God-image in the psyche is not purely good.** Dreams, visions, and encounters with the numinous include **terror, wrath, and destruction**.
2. **The biblical God is morally ambiguous.** Yahweh floods the earth, demands child sacrifice (Isaac), tests Job cruelly, hardens Pharaoh's heart to justify plagues.
3. **Splitting the God-image creates projection.** If God is only good, all evil is projected onto Satan, the world, or other humans—leading to scapegoating, holy wars, and moral inflation.

Jung writes in *Answer to Job*[^9]:

> "The encounter with the creature changes the creator... God desires to become man, and for that he chooses, through the Holy Ghost, the creaturely man filled with darkness—the natural man who is tainted with original sin."

**Psychological Meaning:** The God-image **requires consciousness**. God (the unconscious) seeks to become conscious through humanity. Individuation is the process by which **the God-image becomes aware of itself** through the human psyche.

**The Quaternity (4) vs. The Trinity (3):**

Jung observed that **Christian symbolism is based on the number 3** (Trinity: Father, Son, Holy Spirit), which he saw as **incomplete**. The number **4** represents **wholeness** (four elements, four directions, four functions of consciousness).

To complete the Trinity, Jung proposed adding a **fourth element**: either:
- **The Devil** (to include the shadow of God)
- **Mary** (to include the feminine)
- **Matter** (to include the body and earth)

The **quaternity** is the mandala of the Self—the union of all opposites, including the shadow and the feminine.

### 4. Inflation and the Shadow of the Imago Dei

**Ego-Self Identification (Inflation):**

When the ego **identifies with the God-image**, inflation occurs. The individual believes:
- "I am God" (grandiose delusion)
- "I am enlightened" (spiritual bypassing)
- "I am the chosen one" (messianic complex)

Jung writes[^10]:

> "Inflation is a regression of consciousness into unconsciousness. This happens whenever consciousness takes too many unconscious contents upon itself and loses the faculty of discrimination."

**Forms of Inflation:**
1. **The Guru Complex:** The spiritual teacher who accepts projection and believes they are divine.
2. **The Messiah Complex:** The political or religious leader who believes they are the savior.
3. **The Enlightened One:** The seeker who claims to have transcended the ego and become pure consciousness.
4. **The God-Almighty:** The individual who believes they possess ultimate knowledge or power.

**The Corrective: The Ego-Self Axis:**

Healthy relationship with the Imago Dei requires maintaining the **ego-Self axis**—a conscious connection between the finite ego and the infinite Self/God-image.

- The ego is **not** the Self.
- The ego can **serve** the Self but cannot **become** it.
- The ego must remain **differentiated** while honoring the Self's supremacy.

Jung: *"The self is our life's goal, for it is the completest expression of that fateful combination we call individuality."* The goal is **not to be God** but to **embody the God-image** consciously.

### 5. Integration and Individuation

**The God-Image as the Goal:**

Individuation is the process of realizing the Self/God-image within. Jung writes[^11]:

> "The self is not only the centre but also the whole circumference which embraces both conscious and unconscious; it is the centre of this totality, just as the ego is the centre of consciousness."

**Practical Steps:**
1. **Withdraw Projections:** Recognize the God-image is not "out there" (in teachers, institutions, the beloved) but **within**.
2. **Accept Totality:** Integrate the shadow of the God-image—acknowledge that the divine includes destruction, wrath, and mystery.
3. **Maintain Differentiation:** The ego serves the Self but does not dissolve into it (ego-Self axis).
4. **Live the Question:** The God-image is **unknowable**. Live with the mystery rather than claiming certainty.

**Jung's Final Word:**

In *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, Jung reflects on his life's work[^12]:

> "The decisive question for man is: Is he related to something infinite or not? That is the telling question of his life. Only if we know that the thing which truly matters is the infinite can we avoid fixing our interest upon futilities."

The Imago Dei is that **infinite something**—the numinous center that gives life meaning, purpose, and depth.

- **Complexio Oppositorum** — The union of all opposites in the God-image
- **Answer to Job** — Jung's confrontation with the dark side of God

---

## Closing: Jung's Vision

*"The idea of God is an absolutely necessary psychological function of an irrational nature, which has nothing whatever to do with the question of God's existence. The human intellect can never answer this question, still less give any proof of God. Moreover, such proof is superfluous, for the idea of an all-powerful divine Being is present everywhere, unconsciously if not consciously, because it is an archetype."*[^13]

— C.G. Jung, *Psychology and Religion* (CW 11)

---

*The Imago Dei is not a belief but an experience—the encounter with totality, the sacred center, the infinite within the finite. To know it is to stand in relationship with the mystery that cannot be named, only lived.*
